Everyone's talking about AI for coding. Nobody's talking about what it does for the manager running the team.

Yes, AI makes you a better engineer. But here's where it's moved the needle across the full stack of engineering leadership:

**Sprint planning** used to eat half the day. Now: jot down sparks, map them to tickets, generate entire ticket sets wholesale. It detects dependencies, flags priority cycles, and when you're up against a time constraint it helps cut scope intelligently — not arbitrarily. Connected via MCP to search, update, assign, and reprioritize without ever leaving the session.

**Stakeholder reporting** used to mean manually pulling from a dozen places. Now: scrape 20+ tickets, define criteria, get a report shaped for whoever's reading it. End-of-day summaries just happen.

**Architecture and security reviews** — first-pass reviews before committee. Database tradeoffs, stored proc design, materialization decisions — show up prepared without being the DBA or the security engineer.

**Infrastructure** — Terraform, Kubernetes, ClickHouse, GitHub webhooks, DB performance. The bottleneck was never intelligence, it was syntax and context-switching. That's gone.

**Legacy systems** — documenting undocumented codebases, mapping dependencies, generating architecture diagrams. If you've inherited one, you know what this is worth.

Test cases, refactoring, UI mockups before engineering starts. All of it.

---

A note to engineers: hook your troubleshooting sessions into your Linear or Jira tickets. Capture findings, reasoning, execution plan as you go. Your ticket becomes a living record. "What did you do today?" answers itself. Documentation stops being overhead — it becomes a byproduct of how you work.

---

Something not enough people are talking about: explore what MCPs you actually have access to. Most people haven't looked. MCP connects your AI to your real tools — Linear, GitHub, Slack, your databases — natively. Build a handful of workflows you trigger on the flick of a finger and run every single day.

The real benefit isn't speed. It's communication — richer updates, preserved context, nothing lost. When EOY review comes around you have a trail. A story. Evidence.

For engineers: "what did you do this quarter?" answered with receipts. For managers: "what did your team deliver?" answered with confidence. For introverts who do the deepest work but hate self-promoting — it speaks for you. Quietly. Continuously. Without performing.
